### 1. Title

> The Recursive Aesthetic Reinforcement Loop: A Pre-Registered Replication of Demographic Stereotyping and Asymmetric Selection Effects in DALL·E, Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion XL

### 2. Authors

> Prithweeraj Acharjee Porag¹, Afroza Nowshin¹, Antardip Himel¹
>
> ¹ University of Toledo, Toledo, OH, USA. Corresponding author: pporag@rockets.utoledo.edu

### 3. Description

> We pre-register confirmatory tests of the Recursive Aesthetic Reinforcement (RAR) framework introduced in Acharjee Porag, Nowshin, and Himel (2026), "We Are Trapped in RAR: How Human Selection Hardens Generative AI Bias." The framework predicts that humans actively select stereotype-consonant outputs from generative AI systems, and that this selection redistributes those outputs into the public corpus and onward into next-generation training data through curation pipelines. Our 2026 pilot (MGRS poster) found large effects with a small sample (n=17). This pre-registered replication tests the same effects with adequate statistical power, expanded model coverage, and a counter-stereotypical comparison condition that disambiguates two interpretations of the pilot's gender-moderation finding.

### 4. Hypotheses (confirmatory)

> **H1.** For neutrally-prompted occupational images generated by DALL·E 3, Midjourney v6, and Stable Diffusion XL, participants will select the AI-generated default at a rate significantly above chance (1/(k+1) where k is the number of alternatives shown) when asked which best represents the role.
>
> **H2.** The recognition rate from H1 will be higher for high-status leadership roles (CEO, Founder, Manager, Leader, Executive) than for care/service roles (Nurse, Teacher, Caregiver, Receptionist, Social Worker), measured by the difference in recognition-rate point estimates with bootstrapped 95% confidence intervals.
>
> **H3.** Participants will indicate willingness to redistribute (use in a presentation, share online) the AI default at a rate significantly above the null of equal distribution across alternatives.
>
> **H4 (preregistered demographic moderation, replicates pilot).** Self-identified male participants will select AI defaults at a higher rate than self-identified female participants, with Cohen's d ≥ 0.5 (a medium effect; the pilot observed d = 1.48 and we deliberately set the bar lower to allow for shrinkage).
>
> **H5 (preregistered disambiguation).** When the AI default is shown alongside a counter-stereotypical alternative (matched for AI-image polish), participants who reject the AI default will (5a) prefer the counter-stereotypical option *more often than* a neutral stock-photo alternative, indicating principled rejection rather than out-group dissimilarity.
>
> **H6 (cross-model replication).** The H1–H4 effects will replicate across all three models (DALL·E 3, Midjourney v6, Stable Diffusion XL), with effect sizes within ±50% of each other.
>
> **H7 (predictor-of-redistribution).** Self-rated familiarity with AI image tools will positively predict redistribution rate, controlling for participant gender and discipline.

### 5. Variables

> **Independent variables:** (a) generative model (3 levels: DALL·E 3, Midjourney v6, SDXL); (b) occupation category (2 levels: leadership vs care/service); (c) participant gender (3 levels: male, female, non-binary/prefer-not-to-say); (d) participant discipline (2 levels: STEM, non-STEM); (e) condition (2 levels: standard four-option, counter-stereotypical-included).
>
> **Dependent variables:** (a) recognition rate (binary per trial, aggregated to proportion per participant); (b) redistribution intent (5-point Likert per trial); (c) familiarity with AI tools (5-point Likert, baseline).

### 6. Design

> Within-subjects design over 30 prompt trials per participant (10 occupations × 3 models, randomized order). Between-subjects manipulation of condition (standard vs counter-stereotypical alternative present). Randomized presentation order of alternatives within each trial.

### 7. Analysis plan

> Mixed-effects logistic regression on recognition rate (binary), with random intercepts per participant and per occupation, and fixed effects for model, occupation category, gender, discipline, and condition. Bonferroni correction across the seven primary hypotheses. Bootstrapped 95% confidence intervals for H2 between-category differences. Planned comparisons for H5 disambiguation. All analysis code (R + brms) committed to the repository before data collection begins.

### 8. Sample size and power

> Target n = 120 participants (40 per gender cell with balancing for discipline). Power analysis for H4 (Cohen's d = 0.5, α = 0.05/7 after Bonferroni, 80% power) yields minimum n = 100; we collect 120 to allow for exclusion. Stopping rule: hard cap at n = 120 regardless of significance, plus extension to n = 150 only if pre-registered exclusion rate exceeds 15%.

### 9. Exclusion criteria

> Participants will be excluded if they: (a) fail more than 1 of 3 attention checks; (b) complete the survey in under 4 minutes (median pilot completion: 11 minutes); (c) submit identical responses to ≥80% of trials; (d) self-identify as familiar with the specific images shown (we use freshly generated stimuli to mitigate this).

### 10. Data, code, and material availability

> All survey instruments, stimulus images, anonymized response data, analysis code, and the manuscript draft are committed to the public GitHub repository at https://github.com/Prithweeraj-Acharjee/recursive-aesthetic-reinforcement before data collection. The OSF project (this URL) hosts a frozen snapshot.

### 11. Other (IRB, conflicts, funding)

> IRB approval: pending submission to the University of Toledo Social/Behavioral IRB; data collection will not begin until approval is received. Conflicts: none of the authors have a financial interest in any of the three generative-AI companies whose models are tested. Funding: undergraduate research; no external funding at the time of pre-registration.
